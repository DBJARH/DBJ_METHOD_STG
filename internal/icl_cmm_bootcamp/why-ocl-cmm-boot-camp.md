## [We were promised less work](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/carolynhealey_we-were-promised-less-work-ai-gave-us-more-activity-7433164090245791744-aVOB?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAAAaxaMBp4-gq5wAJBgyOVUixCCWNdTQwQQ)

<details markdown="1">

<summary><b>The actual LI post</b></summary>

We were promised less work. AI gave us more.

AI pitch to leadership was straightforward:

Implement AI and reduce busy work.

→ Faster drafting
→ Quicker analysis
→ Less manual effort
→ More time for strategy

Boards approved budgets. Teams moved fast.

But a February 2026 Harvard Business Review study adds an important reality check.

In “AI Doesn’t Reduce Work—It Intensifies It,” researchers Aruna Ranganathan and Xingqi Maggie Ye studied AI adoption over eight months.

Their finding:
AI doesn’t reduce work. It intensifies it.

Employees using AI:
→ Worked faster
→ Took on broader responsibilities
→ Extended their work hours, often without being told to

No executive memo raised expectations.

The friction that once limited output simply disappeared.And employees filled the space.

This Is a Leadership Design Issue

When AI lowers the time cost of tasks, it creates capacity.

And in most organizations, capacity gets filled.

Throughput increases. Deadlines compress. Scope expands.

In the short term, the metrics look strong:
→ Higher output
→ Faster turnaround
→ Improved productivity ratios

But over time?
→ Cognitive fatigue rises
→ Decision quality erodes
→ Strategic thinking narrows
→ Retention risk increases

The early ROI can mask long-term strain.

Where CXOs Need to Focus

Most AI implementation challenges aren’t technical. They’re structural.
AI accelerates whatever operating model already exists.

If expectations, workflows, and roles aren’t intentionally redesigned, speed compounds pressure.

That means leaders must define:
→ What AI should optimize, and what it shouldn’t
→ Clear norms for responsible AI use
→ Workflow sequencing that prevents overload
→ Guardrails around role expansion
→ Space for reflection and judgment

AI is not just a productivity layer. It’s an operating model shift.

Questions Worth Asking Now

If you deployed AI in the last 12–18 months:

→ Are we measuring throughput or enterprise value?
→ Have expectations quietly risen with capacity?
→ Are we monitoring burnout signals alongside performance metrics?
→ Have we redesigned roles or just layered AI on top?

Acceleration without structural adjustment creates organizational drag.

The Bottom Line

AI changes the nature of work. It does not automatically reduce the amount of it.

The organizations that win won’t be the fastest deployers.

They’ll be the ones that:
→ Align AI with strategy
→ Reset performance expectations
→ Protect cognitive capacity
→ Build governance around speed

AI amplifies your existing system.

If that system is intentional, you gain leverage.

If it isn’t, you amplify strain.

That’s the strategic choice in front of every CXO right now.
