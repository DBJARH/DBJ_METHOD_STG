The concept is clean. Here's the model:

Two landscapes — **Business** and **Technology** — exist independently but must be kept consistent. EA is the alignment function, not a mediator between them, but the discipline that ensures both are coherent with each other and consolidated internally.

![alt text](image.png)


The model has three obligations:

1. **Business Landscape** declares intent — strategy, products, capabilities, and accountability. It must be internally consolidated: no duplicate ownership, no conflicting product definitions, no orphaned processes.

2. **Technology Landscape** implements what business declares — applications, data, infrastructure, and operations. It too must be internally consolidated: rationalised application portfolio, no redundant platforms, consistent standards.

3. **EA** (EA's role) sits between them with three specific duties:
   - **Align** — every technology component traces back to a declared business need; every business capability has a technology realisation
   - **Consolidate** — enforce reduction of redundancy in both landscapes; this is ongoing governance, not a one-time exercise
   - **Govern** — the dashed feedback arrows show that EA is bidirectional — technology constraints inform what business can declare, and business change drives technology evolution

>[!NOTE] BPT key principle: *business declares the product, technology implements it*. EA ensures that chain is intact, traceable, and free of drift in both directions.
